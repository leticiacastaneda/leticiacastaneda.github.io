Leticia Castañeda

Pedagogue · Instructional Design · Organizational Development

I started at 18 in an English school, with no experience and a lot of curiosity. That's where I discovered that teaching isn't about transferring information — it's about provoking something in another person. That conviction has guided every decision in my career since.

Pedagogy took me first through university: as a student, as an intern in the library and educational innovation centers, and then behind the scenes redesigning the curriculum of two undergraduate programs. The next chapter was the corporate world — and with it, a revelation: organizations invest enormously in training, but few stop to ask whether their people are actually learning. That gap — between what's taught and what's learned — became my territory. I've spent eight years working within it, across industries as different as global manufacturing and the tequila industry.

In all those contexts, instructional design has been my core tool. I don't understand it as a linear process of producing materials — I understand it as a creative problem: who will learn this, what do they need to transform, and what's the most honest and effective way to get there?

Sometimes that means a digital ecosystem of 150 curated resources for senior leaders. Other times it means turning a company's Code of Ethics — a document nobody read — into a Jumanji-style game that got 1,500 people to read it, understand it, and actually enjoy it. The format is always secondary. The question that matters is whether something really changed afterward.

I work with multiple methodologies — ADDIE, Design Thinking, accelerated learning, Flipped Classroom, behaviorism, cognitivism, connectivism — not out of eclecticism, but because pedagogy can't be rigid: it adapts to who's learning. Right now I'm in a debate that fascinates me: whether Bloom's Taxonomy needs updating in light of artificial intelligence's influence on younger generations. That tension between the established and the emerging is, for me, where the most interesting work in this field lives.

What people tell me after going through my programs is that something shifts — a perspective changes, a decision gets made, a phrase sticks. A manager who still uses an idea from one of my sessions months later. A colleague who, after a course on decision-making, called back her recruiters and accepted a job offer she'd been putting off. Those moments aren't something I chase — they arrive. And when they do, they remind me why I chose this path and why I can't imagine being anywhere else.
