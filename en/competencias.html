Case study · Competency models

Two models, two worlds: inheriting a framework vs. building one from scratch

The same underlying problem — organizations needing to name and develop the skills they expect from their people — solved in two radically different contexts. In one, I executed and scaled an ambiguous corporate mandate. In the other, I participated in the genesis of the model and built, from scratch, the entire system to bring it to the organization.

Industries Global manufacturing · Tequila industry
My role Instructional design · Organizational development
Scope From Guadalajara to 3 countries · Live system in production

What I learned crossing both worlds

Global manufacturing

I learned to scale. When a design works and is well documented, it travels — from Guadalajara to Chihuahua and Monterrey, and from there to teams in Asia and the United States. I also learned to train trainers: validating someone else's instructional design is a different skill from creating your own.

Tequila industry

I learned to build the method when none exists. Participating in the genesis of a model — not just implementing it — requires a different level of judgment: deciding which content deserves trust, which platform to use without prior experience, and how to turn a curriculum into an experience people actually want to go through.

In both cases, the real constraint was never the budget — it was knowledge. In manufacturing, I didn't know enough about competencies; I researched until I did. In the tequila industry, I didn't know the technical platform the project needed; I learned on the fly, with the help of artificial intelligence, under the same time pressure any real operation imposes. The difference between a project that stays an intention and one that actually reaches people is almost always a willingness to learn what you don't yet know.
